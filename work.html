Work

My research interest started with my undergraduate thesis on the gender pay gap in the livestreaming industry. That project made me realize how powerful—and fun—it can be to play with data and explore fascinating economic questions. At Berkeley, I’ve expanded those skills by applying machine learning and statistical methods to a range of real-world problems, from housing displacement and health risks to social trust and digital sentiment. You can find more of my projects below.

Gender Pay Gap in Livestreaming Industry

I explored income inequality in livestream e-commerce using a dataset of over two million sessions. After cleaning the data and building regression models, I found that women, despite being more active on the platform, earned significantly less than men. The gap narrowed with more followers, livestreaming experience, and MCN (talent agency) support. I also discovered a “same-gender audience effect”: female influencers tend to attract more female viewers, which boosts their sales.

Forecasting Political Violence in Myanmar

I explored conflict escalation in Myanmar using a combination of Difference-in-Differences analysis and machine learning forecasting. The timeline plot reveals a sharp increase in one-sided violence following the 2017 clearance operation, suggesting a break from prior trends. Province-level heatmaps highlight how Rakhine and Kachin states disproportionately bear the burden of conflict. By modeling weekly fatalities and training a Random Forest on pre-policy data, I showed that post-2017 violence was both unpredicted and regime-induced.

Displacement Risk and Housing Price Volatility

In this project, I analyzed how rental patterns and price volatility are connected across California counties during the 2007–2009 housing crisis. I used ACS Census data and home price indices to examine how the distribution of rental rates differed in high-volatility vs. low-volatility regions. Using density plots and choropleth maps, I found that areas with greater price declines tended to show distinct rental rate profiles, offering clues into housing precarity and displacement risks during economic shocks.

User Sentiment and Personalization on Sharpen AI

I analyzed user engagement and sentiment on Sharpen®, a digital mental health platform, to inform personalized content delivery. Using text vectorization and K-means clustering, I segmented over 700 learning modules into thematic categories and applied age-based heterogeneity analysis to examine content preferences. Results showed significant variation in engagement across demographic groups, with users aged 24–35 exhibiting higher responsiveness to mindfulness and clinical modules.

Analyzing Supply Chain Disclosures

In this project, I used Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to extract supply chain–related themes from 10-K filings. After downloading filings through the SEC API and cleaning the raw text, I applied unsupervised topic modeling to identify patterns in how companies report their supply chain risks. I visualized the output using pyLDAvis, revealing which terms dominate each topic and how topics cluster. This approach enabled a document-level score of disclosure relevance and highlighted the limitations of generic topic models in surfacing targeted disclosures.

Machine Learning Prediction Projects

Below are some of the machine learning projects I’ve built, covering prediction tasks in structured data, image recognition, and natural language processing. These projects helped me deepen my understanding of model evaluation, feature engineering, and explainability.

Penguin Machine Learning

I worked on a fun little project where I used machine learning to explore patterns in penguin morphology. First, I trained a HistGradientBoostingClassifier to predict species based on physical traits like bill length and flipper size. Then, I used a Neural Network Regressor to estimate body mass. One thing I found interesting was how different combinations of bill and flipper length form distinct zones of predicted weight.

Can We Predict Music Tastes with Data?

I was curious whether it’s possible to predict music preferences using only a song’s acoustic features — things like danceability, energy, and tempo. So I trained a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) classifier to see how well it could distinguish between high- and low-rated songs. After preprocessing and feature selection, the model reached around 72% accuracy on the test set. This made me think about how much of our personal taste can be quantified and predicted — even something as subjective as music.

Trust in Government Across Demographic Groups

Using data from the 2022 General Social Survey, I looked into how trust in the federal government changes with age. After cleaning the data and building variables like gender, age group, and Hispanic identity, I applied propensity score weighting to make the sample better match U.S. population benchmarks. One interesting pattern: people in their late 20s and early 30s tend to be more trusting of the government, while trust drops off among older adults.

Classifying Clothing Images with Neural Networks

I trained a neural network to classify fashion items from the Fashion MNIST dataset using TensorFlow and Keras. The model processes 28×28 grayscale images of clothing, shoes, and accessories, and predicts one of 10 predefined categories. The architecture consists of a flatten layer followed by two dense layers (128 units, ReLU) and a final softmax layer. After training for 10 epochs, the model achieved a test accuracy of around 88%, Working on this gave me hands-on experience with image data and model tuning, and it sparked my interest in building deeper architectures for more complex visual tasks.

Predicting Heart Attack Risk with Logistic Regression

Using a public health dataset, I built a logistic regression model to estimate an individual's risk of heart attack based on clinical indicators such as age, blood pressure, cholesterol, and chest pain type. After data preprocessing and feature engineering, the model was trained and evaluated with metrics including accuracy (~85%), ROC-AUC, and a detailed confusion matrix. This project helped me better understand how interpretable models can be used in real-world medical settings to flag important warning signs early.

Sentiment Classification with NLP

I used natural language processing to analyze drug reviews from Drugs.com. After cleaning and tokenizing the text, I applied TF-IDF and trained a logistic regression model to classify sentiment. I also used SHAP to interpret what the model picked up on—like how certain keywords strongly signal a bad experience.

Predicting Titanic Survival with ML

I joined the Kaggle Titanic Machine Learning competition and built a full pipeline to predict passenger survival using features like age, sex, class, and fare. I trained a Gradient Boosting model and explored feature importance to understand what really mattered. The project gave me solid experience working with structured data, and I ended up placing in the top 20%.
